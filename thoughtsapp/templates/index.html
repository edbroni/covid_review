Some thoughts about Covid-19

February 23, 2021 by Edgar

The COVID-19 is a new spread of a virus, originating in China that has been causing a great number of victims around the world. It's not impossible to imagine that the majority of world population know about the pandemic and unfortunately were affected by it in some way.

In this pandemic scenario, we could ask ourseleves how the chinese disease affect the countries around the world. Is there a bias in COVID-19 cases and deaths? Will countries with better economic conditions suffer less than the other? Will the countries that performed the most tests get lower mortality rates?

Fortunately the information about this pandemic is easy to find on the internet. Here are some dashboards links:

  • Johns Hopkins COVID-19
  • Our World in data
  • Center for disease Control and Prevention - USA
  • Coronavirus Brasil

Below are the links to some databases:

  • Johns Hopkins COVID-19 Data Repository
  • Our World in Data -repository
  • Open Data SUS - Brasil data repository

The first 2 database links aggregate data from different sources and show global data. The last link contains data from Brazil only. The granularity in these examples is the same, daily notifications, but we hope to find more specific information in national databases than in the global database.

We started to dive into the Johns Hopkins and Our World in Data sources and we decided to use the Our World in Data (owid) database because the aggregation of economic information, shows easy access of field descriptions, source of field and could answer the questions made.

Answering the questions

First, just plotting the graph of COVID-19 new cases by day for world notifications.

Fig. 1 - World new cases smoothed

The first analysis will be the GDP per capita x cases per million, for which the data will be grouped by countries (fig 2). There is a point out of the curve and represents the USA, which has a high GDP and a greate number of cases. As the number of cases and GDP by country is very different, the graph is repeated with logarithm of the variables (fig.3).

Fig. 2 - GDP per capita x Cases per million
Fig. 3 - GDP per capita (log) x Cases per million (log)

It's not possible to observe any trend on the data until the logxlog graph is observed. With the fig. 3 is possible to observe a trend indicating the increase of cases with the increase os GDP per capita.

Now analizing cases per milion x development human index (DHI) (fig. 4 and 5) it is necessary to apply the logarithm in the attribute cases per milion due the variation of the number of cases between countries. In this graph the trend is for an increase in cases with an increase in DHI.

Fig. 4 - Development Human Index x Cases per million
Fig. 5 - Development Human Index x Cases per million (log)

Looking at the graphs of deaths per million x GDP per capita (fig.6) and deaths per million x DHI (fig.7) there is the same positive correlation trend at first glance (again deaths per million and GDP per capita are displayed after log).

Fig. 6 - GDP per capita (log) x Deaths per million (log)
Fig. 7 - Development Human Index x Deaths per million (log)

The data about tests applyed are displayed in fig. 8 and 9. The first information retrieved is that, in general, rich countries applied more tests than the poorest countries. The second information is a conclusion of figs. 3, 6 and 8, the large number of tests didn't mean a low number of cases and low number of deaths.

Fig. 8 - Applied Covid-19 tests (log) x Cases per million (log)
Fig. 9 - Applied Covid-19 tests (log) x Deaths per million (log)

One last question that can be asked (among several) concerns the correlation between population density and cases of COVID, it is expected that countries with a high DHI have a high population concentration and a high DHI indicates a high number of cases of COVID-19, ie analyzed in fig. 9. According to this information, it is not possible to observe correlations between population density and COVID-19 cases.

Fig. 9 - Populational density x Cases per million (log)

Although the analysis of fig.9, we cannot assure there is a lack of correlation between populational density and Covid cases because we had made analysis in a country scale and can occur a situation of concentration of people in few cities in a large country.

Conclusions

With this small study we can answer the proposed questions:

  • The number of cases and deaths (per million) is, in general, high in countries with high GDP per capita and high HDI, there is a bias;
  • Rich countries were more affected by COVID-19 than the poorest (in number of cases and deaths per million);
  • In general, the application of tests did not result in the reduction of cases and deaths of Covid-19.

Some points could be better investigate, some conclusions are not the main stream. It's missing something? Do you have some ideas to further investigate? Just let me know!
